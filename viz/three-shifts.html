Three foundational shifts to transform agricultural water management

The report calls for three reinforcing changes in how AWM is planned, financed, and delivered. Together they turn one-off projects into systems that learn, adapt, and scale.

Shift 1

Embrace AWM complexity

Balance people, prosperity, and planet

Treat agricultural water management as a platform for sustainable development, not just a tool to boost production. Connect water, agriculture, environment, and economic growth across local water availability, agricultural systems, institutions, and socioeconomic conditions. Manage uncertainty and competing interests through iterative learning.

Single-objective projects → Integrated, adaptive systems

Shift 2

From hardware to services

Start small, learn fast, transform at scale

Stop measuring success only in pumps, canals, and dams. Measure it in the quality of water service delivered to farms - reliability, equity, productivity per drop. Build accountability into operators, run small experiments, and scale what works. Performance contracts and farmer-facing service standards turn projects into institutions.

Build it and walk away → Reliable, accountable services

Shift 3

End the guesswork

Collect the data and use them

AWM decisions today often rely on fragmented, outdated, or siloed information. Institutionalize the development and use of water informatics: real-time monitoring, satellite-derived indicators (evapotranspiration, soil moisture), basin-level accounts, and tools that put trade-offs in front of decision-makers. Make data the basis for water allocation, not anecdote.

Project-by-project intuition → Continuous, evidence-led management
